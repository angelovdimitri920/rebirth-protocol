# Theme and Premise

*Authoritative creative-identity document. Consolidated baseline: 2026-07-23.*

## 1. Status

This document consolidates the project's existing high-level identity without resolving the decisions listed in Section 16.

- Unless labeled otherwise, Sections 2 through 14 are **Confirmed canon**.
- Section 16 is **Undeveloped** or **Draft** by design.
- Detailed history, technology, factions, characters, campaign, and mechanics belong in Sections 02 through 08.

## 2. Working title

**Rebirth Protocol**

The title has two established meanings:

1. The battlefield rhythm in which a knocked-down harness rises under a brief inviolable flare.
2. The ancient civilization-scale continuity system that may restore, overwrite, merge, or destroy humanity.

The name remains technically a working title, but it is deeply integrated into the game's mechanics, setting, imagery, and central mystery.

## 3. High-level description

**Rebirth Protocol is a single-player, story-driven 3D arena mecha action game with a JRPG-style campaign shell and roguelite tournament events.**

The game combines:

- Modular pre-battle harness construction.
- Real-time 3D arena duels.
- Twenty-to-forty-minute Passage events with run-scoped choices.
- A walkable hub, named characters, shops, dialogue, and story scenes.
- A persistent campaign about inherited power, technological obedience, and the meaning of Rebirth.

The approved structural shorthand is:

> **A JRPG-shaped game with a Western soul.**

This means a JRPG-like campaign shape expressed through chivalric romance, grounded dialogue, political and religious institutions, heraldic ritual, and a neo-feudal used future. It does not mean imitating a specific Japanese role-playing game or reducing Western influence to surface medieval decoration.

## 4. Core premise

Millennia after the **Final Edict** turned the Meridian Ascendancy's command infrastructure against civilization, humanity has rebuilt into competing neo-feudal states organized around inherited knight harnesses.

No living society can manufacture the greatest Firstwork harnesses from raw materials. Great Houses and Orders preserve them as military inheritance. Guild foundries have recently begun producing weaker but replaceable Revival-pattern machines, threatening the hereditary monopoly over harness warfare.

Reconnecting the Meridian system might end humanity's wars. It could also complete the original apocalypse.

## 5. Core player fantasy

The player should feel that they are:

- Becoming an **armiger**, a knighted harness pilot.
- Building a personal 10-to-14-meter knight harness from compatible modular parts.
- Mastering ritualized, readable, real-time arena combat.
- Learning named rivals well enough to defeat their doctrines rather than merely overpower their statistics.
- Claiming laurels, equipment, obligations, and attention through the Grand Passage.
- Rising from an unknown fighter toward the Paragons of the Passage.
- Entering a political and religious struggle over the Final Claimant and Rebirth.
- Deciding what kind of power humanity should preserve, share, refuse, destroy, or complete.

The protagonist's specific origin and motivation remain undeveloped. The broader fantasy is established.

## 6. Core gameplay promise

The campaign loop is:

> **Talk and shop in Lasthold → customize and test the harness → enter a Passage → fight a sequence of duels → receive laurels, spoils, and a story development → return to the world**

Three original pillars govern the experience:

1. **Build expression:** Parts and rewards change how the player fights, not merely numerical strength.
2. **Readable, skill-based combat:** Openings are earned through spacing, baiting, cover, commitment, recovery, and knowledge of the opponent.
3. **One more Passage:** Events offer escalating power, meaningful choices, and legible builds while advancing a persistent campaign.

The combination of modular construction, arena combat, roguelite event structure, and story-driven progression is the project's primary differentiator.

## 7. Signature combat identity

The defining battle rhythm is:

> **Casting → neutral combat → poise break → knockdown → rebirth → renewed neutral**

The presentation and mechanics must reinforce the same principles:

- Commitment buys damage.
- Landing and attack recovery create punish windows.
- Movement, range, and cover matter more than button-mashing.
- Melee, guns, bombs, pods, and shields have distinct tactical jobs.
- Opponents retain readable weaknesses even at high rank.
- Downed harnesses are visually kneeling.
- Rebirth is visually an accolade: the knight rises beneath ancestral godlight.

This kneel-and-accolade pairing is the clearest single fusion of mechanics, world, and visual identity.

## 8. Setting identity

The setting is a **neo-feudal used future**, not conventional medieval fantasy and not a society frozen in time.

Its institutions developed because durable local structures survived the collapse of universal infrastructure:

- Great Houses.
- Military Orders.
- Chartered cities and trade leagues.
- Guilds and foundries.
- Religious jurisdictions and monasteries.
- Mercenary companies and Hedge Errantry.
- Local communities organized around heat, water, food, archives, and inherited machinery.

Religion, law, engineering, inheritance, identity, and political legitimacy form one connected system. A rite may also be a maintenance procedure. A genealogy may determine who can activate a harness. A religious court may decide whether a dead pilot's echo can command the living.

The visual world distinguishes:

- Coherent inherited relics.
- Generational free-company patchwork.
- Honest, reproducible scrapwright work.
- Disturbing Edictbound corruption.

## 9. Central thematic question

> **Can humanity safely possess power that only works through obedience?**

Every major ideology provides a different answer:

- The Universal Communion requires power to remain Severable and capable of refusal.
- The Rite of the Whole Crown believes a fully authenticated sovereign can make absolute command lawful.
- The Covenant of the Second Sun judges power by whether ordinary communities can understand, reproduce, govern, and stop it.
- The Court of Ten Thousand Banners binds power to remembered obligation and lineage.
- The Broken Choir wants to complete the transformation begun by the Final Edict.
- The Ash Levelers want to destroy the harness order with the forbidden power it monopolizes.

## 10. Supporting themes

- Authority versus refusal.
- Power versus consent.
- Restoration versus restraint.
- Inherited privilege versus reproducible technology.
- Tradition versus political reform.
- Memory versus identity.
- The rights of the living versus the authority of the dead.
- Whether a machine can become a person through refusal.
- Whether technological dependence inevitably becomes political obedience.
- Whether peace imposed by absolute coordination is still peace.
- Understanding technology versus merely possessing it.
- Honor and ritual as imperfect restraints on violence.
- Rebirth as restoration, resurrection, replacement, merger, or loss of self.
- Humanity's ability to rebuild without repeating earlier systems of domination.

## 11. Central conflicts

### Political

Great Houses and old Orders face guilds, trade states, mercenaries, and reform movements empowered by mass-producible Revival harnesses after the War of the Open Forges.

### Religious and ideological

The Communion, Whole Crown, Second Sun, Banner Court, Broken Choir, and Ash Levelers disagree over sovereignty, consent, inheritance, machine personhood, and Rebirth.

### Personal

**Draft:** The protagonist may carry an identity, component, lineage, or authentication state connected to the Final Claimant. Major powers could seek to protect, control, study, transform, recruit, or kill them.

### Moral

Edictbound equipment offers immediate capability at the cost of dishonor, contamination, memory loss, identity collapse, and eventual service to the Final Edict. The player should confront useful forbidden power, not a simplistic evil-item choice.

## 12. Current story promise

The locked campaign spine is:

> **Circuit → Broken Choir → First Armiger → Cradle**

The story begins inside a public, ritualized tournament culture and gradually reveals the political and existential purpose behind the Grand Passage.

**Draft:** Riderless waits in the Cradle as an opponent and mirror of the protagonist. Both rise through Rebirth, but Riderless may understand what the Protocol restores.

Specific acts, endings, protagonist revelations, and the exact final confrontation are not yet confirmed.

## 13. Tone

The intended tone is:

- Solemn without being constantly miserable.
- Ceremonial and chivalric.
- Politically and religiously serious.
- Melancholic about lost civilization.
- Suspicious of easy restoration.
- Visually majestic but materially worn.
- Grounded in practical survival and human institutions.
- Capable of warmth, humor, rivalry, and tournament spectacle without becoming parody.

The tonal shorthand is:

> **Cathedral relics, working foundries, heraldic arena combat, and an apocalypse still waiting for authorization.**

## 14. Visual and audio identity

The high-level identity is:

- Giant knight harnesses rather than generic robots.
- Faceless helms or helm-like cockpit forms, dominant pauldrons, layered skirts, heraldic panels, and readable armor silhouettes.
- Carried or deliberately mounted arms that reinforce knightly combat language.
- Heroic proportions and visibly weighty movement.
- Warm gold-white godlight for lawful ancestral technology.
- Violet-black reserved for Edictbound systems and Riderless.
- Forge sparks, bell-like impacts and explosions, plainchant influence, anvil percussion, and the four-note Hush motif.
- Heraldry, repairs, and damage that reveal ownership history.

Detailed and currently authoritative visual rules remain in [`ART_DIRECTION.md`](ART_DIRECTION.md). The universal anatomy of the recently explored headless knight-helm cockpit has not yet replaced that document's separate sensor-helm rule.

### Recently approved chassis concept

**Draft as a setting-wide rule:** One approved visual concept establishes a headless knight harness with:

- A torso-cockpit shaped as the knight's helm, with the pilot housed inside it.
- No separate robot head.
- No humanoid hands.
- A right arm formed around an integrated lance.
- A left arm formed as the shield itself.
- Grey knightly plate with comparatively simple, Gundam-like surface treatment.
- Pauldrons and greaves shaped more directly from historical knight armor.

The concept is an approved direction for that specific machine. Whether any of these features define every harness, one chassis family, or a singular named harness remains **Undeveloped**. Until Section 03 and `ART_DIRECTION.md` are deliberately revised, do not use it to erase carried weapons, hand-mounted parts, or separate sensor-helms from other chassis.

## 15. Creative non-negotiables

- The game cannot become a button-mashing arena brawler.
- Parts must alter playstyle, not merely increase statistics.
- Any lawful part can be used with any compatible chassis.
- Every chassis must express a real movement and combat archetype.
- Gun, melee, bomb, shield, and pod-centered builds must remain viable.
- Religion must affect law, inheritance, infrastructure, and technology.
- Edictbound is a command infection, not a manufacturing era or ordinary equipment faction.
- Historical cultures are design inspiration, not literal surviving Earth civilizations.
- No design may directly reproduce a named Custom Robo machine, part, arena, or character.
- Inherited, salvaged, reproducible, and corrupted technology must remain visually distinct.
- The final campaign cannot be framed as a menu-only roguelite.
- Central mysteries cannot receive premature definitive answers.
- Riderless and Edictbound imagery must remain exceptional rather than commonplace.
- The world must remain understandable through human institutions, material needs, and consequences, even when its deepest technology is mysterious.
